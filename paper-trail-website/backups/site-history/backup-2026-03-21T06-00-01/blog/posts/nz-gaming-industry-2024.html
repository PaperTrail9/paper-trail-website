Why New Zealand's Mobile Game Industry is Poised for a Breakthrough in 2024

With the NZ mobile gaming market projected to reach $107.5 million this year, local developers have a unique opportunity. Here's what the data tells us about where the industry is headed—and how Kiwi creators can capture this growing market.

The New Zealand game development scene has grown exponentially, with studios now contributing over $759 million annually to the economy. Photo: NZGDA

The numbers tell a compelling story. According to Statista's latest market forecast, New Zealand's mobile gaming sector is on track to generate US$107.50 million in revenue during 2024. That's not just pocket change—it's a clear signal that our little corner of the world is becoming a serious player in the global gaming economy.

But here's what makes this opportunity particularly interesting for Kiwi developers: we're not just participating in this growth—we're positioned to lead it. The combination of our unique cultural perspective, world-class technical talent, and the increasing accessibility of game development tools has created a perfect storm for New Zealand's mobile gaming renaissance.

The Global Context: Mobile Gaming's Unstoppable Rise

Before diving into what makes New Zealand special, let's look at the bigger picture. According to Udonis Mobile Marketing's comprehensive 2024 report, the global gaming industry generated more than $187.7 billion in revenue last year, with mobile gaming representing the fastest-growing segment at +2.1% year-over-year. What's particularly noteworthy is that mobile gaming now accounts for approximately 49% of the entire global games market—nearly half of all gaming revenue worldwide.

"Mobile gaming isn't just the future of gaming—it's the present. More people play games on their phones than on all other platforms combined."

What's driving this growth? Several factors: the ubiquity of smartphones, improvements in mobile hardware that rival last-generation consoles, and the rise of "hyper-casual" games that anyone can pick up and play for five minutes. The barrier to entry has never been lower, and the potential audience has never been larger. In fact, Newzoo's 2024 Global Games Market Report indicates there are now over 3.38 billion mobile gamers worldwide—a figure that represents nearly 42% of the global population.

Mobile gaming revenue has grown consistently year-over-year, with 2024 projected to reach $98.7 billion globally. Source: Newzoo 2024

New Zealand's Secret Weapons

So why should Kiwi developers be particularly excited? Let's break down the advantages unique to our position:

1. The "Kiwi Creative" Advantage

New Zealand has always punched above its weight in creative industries. From Peter Jackson's film empire to Lorde's global music success, we've consistently shown that our unique perspective resonates worldwide. The NZ Game Developers Association's 2024-2025 annual survey revealed record-breaking results: $759 million in revenue and 1,418 jobs across the sector—a remarkable 34% increase from the previous year.

This creative DNA translates perfectly to mobile gaming. The most successful mobile games aren't just technically proficient—they're emotionally resonant. They tell stories, create moments, and build communities. That's where Kiwi developers excel. Our isolation has bred a distinct storytelling tradition, one that combines understated humor with deep emotional authenticity—a combination that global audiences increasingly crave.

2. Technical Excellence Meets Practical Constraints

Mobile game development requires a unique mindset. Unlike PC or console development, where you can throw hardware at problems, mobile developers must optimize for limited processing power, battery life, and varying network conditions. This constraint-based creativity is something New Zealand developers understand intuitively—we've always had to do more with less.

Our universities are producing world-class graduates in computer science and digital design. Institutions like the University of Auckland, Victoria University of Wellington, and Media Design School have developed specialized game development programs that rival those in much larger countries. These programs don't just teach technical skills—they emphasize the entrepreneurial mindset necessary to succeed in the competitive mobile gaming market.

3. The Time Zone Trading Advantage

Here's a factor many overlook: New Zealand's time zone is perfect for the global gaming market. When we start our workday, the US West Coast is just finishing theirs. When we wrap up, Europe is coming online. This creates a natural 24-hour development cycle that can dramatically accelerate iteration and testing. A bug reported by American players in the evening can be fixed by Kiwi developers the next morning, before European players even wake up.

New Zealand's Thriving Game Development Ecosystem

To understand why New Zealand is positioned for a breakthrough, you need to understand the ecosystem that has developed here. We're not talking about a handful of indie developers working in isolation—we're talking about a mature, interconnected industry with global reach.

The Heavy Hitters

Let's start with the studios that have already proven New Zealand can compete on the world stage. Grinding Gear Games, based in Auckland, has built Path of Exile into one of the most successful action RPGs in the world, with over 25 million registered players. Their success has created a ripple effect, with former employees founding their own studios and raising the bar for the entire industry.

PikPok, another Auckland-based studio, has been creating mobile games since 1997 and has shipped over 100 titles. Their Into the Dead series has been downloaded more than 150 million times worldwide. They've demonstrated that Kiwi studios can consistently produce mobile hits that compete with products from much larger companies.

Studio Thunderhorse in Wellington has carved out a niche creating narrative-driven mobile experiences that showcase the storytelling prowess New Zealand is famous for. Their approach proves that mobile games can be artistic statements, not just time-killers.

Major game development studios are concentrated in Auckland and Wellington, with emerging hubs in Christchurch and Dunedin. Source: NZGDA Industry Survey 2024

The Rising Stars

Beyond the established players, a new generation of Kiwi studios is emerging. Runaway Play, based in Dunedin, has built a loyal following with their nature-themed mobile games that feel distinctly New Zealand. Squid Squad in Wellington is pushing boundaries with innovative AR experiences. Balancing Monkey Games is creating cooperative multiplayer experiences that emphasize the social connections that make gaming meaningful.

What connects all these studios is a willingness to experiment. New Zealand's small market size means developers must think globally from day one, but our creative culture means we're not afraid to take risks that larger, more conservative markets might avoid.

The Technology Stack Question

One of the most common questions we hear from aspiring Kiwi game developers is: "What engine should I use?" The answer, as always, depends on your specific needs—but here are the key players in 2024:

Unity remains the dominant force, powering roughly 50% of all mobile games. Its asset store ecosystem and cross-platform capabilities make it the safe choice for most projects. However, the recent runtime fee controversy has many developers exploring alternatives. Despite this, Unity's dominance in mobile remains largely unchallenged for now.

Unreal Engine has made significant strides in mobile optimization. While traditionally associated with high-end PC and console games, Unreal 5's mobile renderer can produce stunning results—if you have the technical expertise to optimize it properly. For Kiwi studios aiming for premium visual experiences, it's become a serious contender.

Godot has emerged as the indie darling. Completely free and open-source, it's gaining traction among Kiwi developers who want full control without licensing headaches. For 2D games especially, it's become a serious contender. The Godot community in New Zealand is particularly active, with regular meetups and knowledge-sharing sessions.

React Native / Flutter might seem like odd choices for games, but for simple casual games or games with heavy UI components, they can dramatically accelerate development. At Paper Trail, we've used React Native for several game-adjacent projects with great results.

Market Trends and Statistics: The Full Picture

Let's dive deeper into the numbers that matter. Understanding the market dynamics is crucial for any developer looking to capture their share of that $107.5 million pie.

Demographics: Who's Playing?

The stereotype of the teenage boy gamer is officially dead. In New Zealand, mobile gamers span all demographics. According to recent data, women represent 52% of mobile gamers in the Asia-Pacific region (which includes New Zealand), and the fastest-growing demographic is players over 35. The average mobile gaming session lasts about 25 minutes, with most players engaging multiple times per day.

Perhaps most interesting for Kiwi developers: New Zealand players show higher-than-average engagement with narrative-driven and artistic games. Our local market may be small, but it serves as an excellent testing ground for games that can resonate globally.

Genre Performance: What's Working?

Not all game genres are created equal in the mobile space. In 2024, the top-performing genres by revenue are:

  • RPGs (Role-Playing Games): 23% of mobile game revenue—driven by gacha mechanics and deep progression systems
  • Strategy games: 17% of revenue, particularly 4X strategy and tower defense titles
  • Puzzle games: 15% of revenue, led by match-3 and word games with broad demographic appeal
  • Action/Shooter: 14% of revenue, including battle royale titles that have found huge mobile success
  • Simulation: 12% of revenue, from life sims to vehicle simulations

For Kiwi developers, the opportunity lies in combining these proven genres with unique creative twists. A puzzle game with a distinctly New Zealand aesthetic. A strategy game inspired by Māori mythology. The possibilities are endless.

RPGs and Strategy games dominate mobile revenue in New Zealand, but Puzzle games show the highest player retention rates. Source: App Annie / data.ai 2024

Platform Split: iOS vs Android

In New Zealand, the platform split differs slightly from global averages. iOS holds a 58% market share versus Android's 42%, largely due to higher average income levels and Apple's strong presence in the local market. This has implications for monetization: iOS users typically spend 2.5x more on in-app purchases than Android users, though Android offers greater reach through its larger install base.

The smart strategy? Develop for both, but prioritize iOS for initial soft launches and premium monetization features. Many successful Kiwi studios follow this pattern, using New Zealand and Australia as test markets before global release.

Monetization: The Revenue Reality

Let's talk money. The $107.5 million figure represents total market revenue—but how does that actually break down for developers? According to industry data, the most successful monetization strategies for Kiwi mobile games in 2024 are:

  • Free-to-play with in-app purchases (IAP): Still the dominant model, representing roughly 75% of mobile game revenue. The key is designing compelling value propositions that feel fair to players
  • Ad-supported: Growing rapidly, especially for hyper-casual games. Rewarded video ads (watch an ad, get a reward) show the best user retention and are preferred by 62% of players over interstitial ads
  • Subscription models: Emerging as a viable option for games with ongoing content updates. Apple Arcade and Google Play Pass are changing how players discover premium experiences
  • Premium upfront: Rare but not dead—works best for narrative-driven or unique gameplay experiences. The resurgence of "premium" mobile games on subscription platforms has breathed new life into this model

The key insight? Successful Kiwi games typically combine multiple monetization strategies. The "whale" model (relying on a small percentage of high-spending users) is becoming less viable as users become more sophisticated and regulatory scrutiny increases. Sustainable revenue comes from broad appeal and ethical monetization that respects players' time and money.

Government Support and Industry Initiatives

New Zealand's game development industry doesn't exist in a vacuum—it's supported by proactive government policies and industry organizations that recognize its potential. The New Zealand Film Commission now includes interactive media in its funding scope, acknowledging that games are the storytelling medium of the 21st century.

Callaghan Innovation, the government's innovation agency, offers R&D grants specifically tailored to game development studios. These grants can cover up to 20% of R&D expenditure, making a significant difference for studios pushing technical boundaries.

The NZ Game Developers Association continues to be the heartbeat of the community, organizing events like the annual NZ Game Developers Conference, running mentorship programs, and advocating for industry-friendly policies. Their work has been instrumental in establishing New Zealand as a credible player in the global gaming market.

The Path Forward: Practical Advice for Kiwi Developers

If you're a New Zealand developer looking to capture a piece of this $107.5 million pie, here's our advice based on both market data and our own experience:

Start Small, Think Global

The most successful mobile games often start as minimum viable products. Build your core loop, test it with real users, iterate based on feedback. Don't try to build a massive RPG as your first project—start with something you can complete in 3-6 months. PikPok's philosophy of "fail fast, learn faster" has served them well over 100+ releases.

Embrace the "Kiwi-ness"

Don't try to make your game feel like it came from Silicon Valley or Tokyo. Lean into what makes New Zealand unique—our landscapes, our humor, our perspective. Games like Path of Exile (developed by Wellington-based Grinding Gear Games) prove that Kiwi flavor can resonate globally. The world's appetite for authentic, diverse voices has never been stronger.

Build Community Early

The NZ Game Developers Association runs regular meetups and events. Get involved. The local game dev community is incredibly supportive, and the connections you make will be invaluable—whether for feedback, collaboration, or just moral support during the inevitable crunch periods. The Kiwi game dev scene is famously collaborative rather than competitive.

Think Beyond the Game

Modern mobile games are as much about community and content as they are about gameplay. Plan your social media strategy, consider how you'll engage with players post-launch, and think about what makes your game shareable. The games that succeed are often the ones that become part of players' social lives. Discord servers, TikTok content, and influencer partnerships are now essential components of a successful launch strategy.

Consider the Hybrid Approach

The lines between mobile, PC, and console gaming are blurring. Many successful Kiwi studios develop for multiple platforms simultaneously, using mobile as both a revenue stream and a marketing funnel for their premium experiences. Cross-platform progression has become an expectation, not a luxury.

Looking Ahead: 2025 and Beyond

The mobile gaming market shows no signs of slowing. With emerging technologies like AR (Apple's Vision Pro, while expensive, points to interesting possibilities) and cloud gaming reducing hardware constraints, the potential for innovative Kiwi developers has never been greater.

But perhaps more importantly, the cultural moment is right. After years of pandemic-induced isolation, people are hungry for connection—and mobile games provide that in spades. Whether it's competing on leaderboards, cooperating in guilds, or just sharing achievements, games have become a primary social platform for millions.

The industry is also maturing in ways that favor thoughtful, sustainable development. The hyper-casual gold rush is showing signs of fatigue, with players increasingly seeking deeper, more meaningful experiences. This shift plays to New Zealand's strengths—we've never been good at shallow, and our best work comes from genuine creative passion.

For New Zealand developers, this represents more than just a business opportunity. It's a chance to put our creative stamp on a global medium, to tell our stories to the world, and to prove once again that from our small corner of the Pacific, we can create things that matter.

The $107.5 million is waiting. The tools have never been more accessible. The audience has never been larger. The only question remaining is: who's going to claim their share?
