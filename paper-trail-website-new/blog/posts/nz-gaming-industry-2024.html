Why New Zealand's Mobile Game Industry is Poised for a Breakthrough in 2024

With the NZ mobile gaming market projected to reach $107.5 million this year, Kiwi developers have a unique opportunity. Here's what the data tells us—and how to capture it.

The numbers tell a compelling story. According to Statista's latest market forecast, New Zealand's mobile gaming sector is on track to generate US$107.50 million in revenue during 2024. That's not just pocket change—it's a clear signal that our little corner of the world is becoming a serious player in the global gaming economy.

But here's what makes this opportunity particularly interesting for Kiwi developers: we're not just participating in this growth—we're positioned to lead it. The combination of our unique cultural perspective, world-class technical talent, and the increasing accessibility of game development tools has created a perfect storm for New Zealand's mobile gaming renaissance.

The Global Context: Mobile Gaming's Unstoppable Rise

Before diving into what makes New Zealand special, let's look at the bigger picture. According to Udonis Mobile Marketing's comprehensive 2024 report, the global gaming industry generated more than $187.7 billion in revenue last year, with mobile gaming representing the fastest-growing segment at +2.1% year-over-year.

"Mobile gaming isn't just the future of gaming—it's the present. More people play games on their phones than on all other platforms combined."

What's driving this growth? Several factors: the ubiquity of smartphones, improvements in mobile hardware that rival last-generation consoles, and the rise of "hyper-casual" games that anyone can pick up and play for five minutes. The barrier to entry has never been lower, and the potential audience has never been larger.

New Zealand's Secret Weapons

So why should Kiwi developers be particularly excited? Let's break down the advantages unique to our position:

1. The "Kiwi Creative" Advantage

New Zealand has always punched above its weight in creative industries. From Peter Jackson's film empire to Lorde's global music success, we've consistently shown that our unique perspective resonates worldwide. The NZ Game Developers Association's 2025 annual survey revealed record-breaking results: $759 million in revenue and 1,418 jobs across the sector.

This creative DNA translates perfectly to mobile gaming. The most successful mobile games aren't just technically proficient—they're emotionally resonant. They tell stories, create moments, and build communities. That's where Kiwi developers excel.

2. Technical Excellence Meets Practical Constraints

Mobile game development requires a unique mindset. Unlike PC or console development, where you can throw hardware at problems, mobile developers must optimize for limited processing power, battery life, and varying network conditions. This constraint-based creativity is something New Zealand developers understand intuitively—we've always had to do more with less.

3. The Time Zone Trading Advantage

Here's a factor many overlook: New Zealand's time zone is perfect for the global gaming market. When we start our workday, the US West Coast is just finishing theirs. When we wrap up, Europe is coming online. This creates a natural 24-hour development cycle that can dramatically accelerate iteration and testing.

The Technology Stack Question

One of the most common questions we hear from aspiring Kiwi game developers is: "What engine should I use?" The answer, as always, depends on your specific needs—but here are the key players in 2024:

Unity remains the dominant force, powering roughly 50% of all mobile games. Its asset store ecosystem and cross-platform capabilities make it the safe choice for most projects. However, the recent runtime fee controversy has many developers exploring alternatives.

Unreal Engine has made significant strides in mobile optimization. While traditionally associated with high-end PC and console games, Unreal 5's mobile renderer can produce stunning results—if you have the technical expertise to optimize it properly.

Godot has emerged as the indie darling. Completely free and open-source, it's gaining traction among Kiwi developers who want full control without licensing headaches. For 2D games especially, it's become a serious contender.

React Native / Flutter might seem like odd choices for games, but for simple casual games or games with heavy UI components, they can dramatically accelerate development. At Paper Trail, we've used React Native for several game-adjacent projects with great results.

Monetization: The Revenue Reality

Let's talk money. The $107.5 million figure represents total market revenue—but how does that actually break down for developers? According to industry data, the most successful monetization strategies for Kiwi mobile games in 2024 are:

  • Free-to-play with in-app purchases (IAP): Still the dominant model, representing roughly 75% of mobile game revenue
  • Ad-supported: Growing rapidly, especially for hyper-casual games. Rewarded video ads (watch an ad, get a reward) show the best user retention
  • Subscription models: Emerging as a viable option for games with ongoing content updates
  • Premium upfront: Rare but not dead—works best for narrative-driven or unique gameplay experiences

The key insight? Successful Kiwi games typically combine multiple monetization strategies. The "whale" model (relying on a small percentage of high-spending users) is becoming less viable as users become more sophisticated. Sustainable revenue comes from broad appeal and ethical monetization.

The Path Forward: Practical Advice for Kiwi Developers

If you're a New Zealand developer looking to capture a piece of this $107.5 million pie, here's our advice based on both market data and our own experience:

Start Small, Think Global

The most successful mobile games often start as minimum viable products. Build your core loop, test it with real users, iterate based on feedback. Don't try to build a massive RPG as your first project—start with something you can complete in 3-6 months.

Embrace the "Kiwi-ness"

Don't try to make your game feel like it came from Silicon Valley or Tokyo. Lean into what makes New Zealand unique—our landscapes, our humor, our perspective. Games like Path of Exile (developed by Wellington-based Grinding Gear Games) prove that Kiwi flavor can resonate globally.

Build Community Early

The NZ Game Developers Association runs regular meetups and events. Get involved. The local game dev community is incredibly supportive, and the connections you make will be invaluable—whether for feedback, collaboration, or just moral support during the inevitable crunch periods.

Think Beyond the Game

Modern mobile games are as much about community and content as they are about gameplay. Plan your social media strategy, consider how you'll engage with players post-launch, and think about what makes your game shareable. The games that succeed are often the ones that become part of players' social lives.

Looking Ahead: 2025 and Beyond

The mobile gaming market shows no signs of slowing. With emerging technologies like AR (Apple's Vision Pro, while expensive, points to interesting possibilities) and cloud gaming reducing hardware constraints, the potential for innovative Kiwi developers has never been greater.

But perhaps more importantly, the cultural moment is right. After years of pandemic-induced isolation, people are hungry for connection—and mobile games provide that in spades. Whether it's competing on leaderboards, cooperating in guilds, or just sharing achievements, games have become a primary social platform for millions.

For New Zealand developers, this represents more than just a business opportunity. It's a chance to put our creative stamp on a global medium, to tell our stories to the world, and to prove once again that from our small corner of the Pacific, we can create things that matter.

The $107.5 million is waiting. Who's going to claim their share?
